How to Choose the Right Running Shoe

Finding the right running shoe can feel overwhelming — shelves are stacked with dozens of options, and marketing jargon doesn’t make things easier. The good news is that shoe choice usually comes down to a few fundamentals: your foot type, your training load, and your personal comfort.

Step 1 — Work out your gait

Your “gait” is how your foot rolls when you run. Most runners fit into one of three categories:

  • Neutral: Foot rolls in naturally. Works well in most neutral trainers.
  • Overpronation: Foot rolls inwards too much. Stability shoes add support here.
  • Supination (underpronation): Foot doesn’t roll enough. Cushioned neutral shoes help absorb shock.

If you’re unsure, check your old shoes — worn-down inner edges usually suggest overpronation.

Step 2 — Match shoe type to training

Different shoes are designed for different purposes. Ideally, you rotate more than one type:

  • Daily trainer: Durable, cushioned, for everyday miles (e.g. Nike Pegasus 41).
  • Speed/tempo shoe: Lightweight, springy, built for fast sessions (e.g. Saucony Endorphin Speed 4).
  • Max cushion: Plush, protective, for long runs (e.g. Brooks Glycerin 21).
  • Stability shoe: Added structure for overpronators (e.g. ASICS Gel-Kayano 31).

Step 3 — Get the fit right

  • Leave a thumb’s width at the front so your toes don’t jam on long runs.
  • Heel should feel locked-in, not sliding.
  • Try shoes in the afternoon or after a run — feet swell through the day.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Buying purely on looks or brand hype.
  • Sticking with the same model every year — updates can change fit.
  • Only owning one pair — rotation extends lifespan and reduces injury risk.

FAQs

  • How many miles do shoes last? Typically 300–500 miles depending on weight, gait and terrain.
  • Do I need carbon plates? Only if chasing speed — most runners benefit more from cushioning and fit.
  • Are more expensive shoes always better? Not necessarily — mid-range models often give the best value for everyday runners.
