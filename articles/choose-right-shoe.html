How to Choose the Right Running Shoe

Affiliate disclosure: As an Amazon Associate I earn from qualifying purchases.

Choosing the right running shoe can feel overwhelming. With hundreds of models on the market in 2025, focus on the big four: gait, cushioning, fit, and intended use. This guide breaks down what matters most — so you can buy with confidence.

Step 1 — Know your gait

Running shoes are grouped as neutral or stability:

  • Neutral: Suits runners whose shoes wear evenly and who don’t overpronate. Examples: Nike Pegasus 41, Brooks Glycerin 21.
  • Stability: For runners whose feet roll inward (overpronate). Examples: ASICS Gel-Kayano 31, Brooks Adrenaline GTS 23.

Tip: If you’re unsure, a quick gait check at a local running shop (or filming your stride on a treadmill) can help.

Step 2 — Match cushioning to training

  • Daily miles: Balanced cushioning that does everything well — e.g. Saucony Ride 17.
  • Long runs & recovery: Softer, higher-stack comfort — e.g. New Balance 1080 v13.
  • Speed sessions: Lighter, more responsive — e.g. Saucony Endorphin Speed 4.
  • Races: Carbon-plated “super shoes” — e.g. Nike Alphafly 3.

Step 3 — Nail the fit

  • A thumb’s width of space at the toes to avoid black toenails.
  • Heel lockdown so there’s no slipping or rubbing.
  • Midfoot snug (not tight). Try shoes with the socks you’ll run in.

Step 4 — Consider a rotation

Two or three complementary shoes share the load, reduce repetitive stress and extend durability. Example rotation:

  • Daily trainer — Nike Pegasus 41
  • Long-run cushion — Brooks Glycerin 21
  • Speed day — Saucony Endorphin Speed 4

Step 5 — Budget smart

Most good running shoes cost £110–£175 in 2025. Last year’s model often offers the best value with minimal differences.

Common mistakes to avoid

  • Buying purely by looks — comfort and fit first.
  • Keeping shoes beyond 500 miles — foam feels flat and form suffers.
  • Assuming the same model will always fit — yearly updates can change shape.

FAQs

  • How long do running shoes last? Typically 300–500 miles depending on foam and your body weight/terrain.
  • Should beginners buy carbon shoes? Not necessary. Build a base in daily trainers; add carbon later for racing.
  • Do expensive shoes last longer? Not always — premium foams can compress at a similar rate.
